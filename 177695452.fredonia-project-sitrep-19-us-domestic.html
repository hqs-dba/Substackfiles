1. Major actions & statements

  1. Trade and economic / tariffs:

On October 1, Trump’s tariffs on imported branded drugs, heavy trucks and furniture took effect. Trump is now selling this as part of the administration’s use of tariffs as a foreign-policy and economic leverage tool. He needs this justification, since prices will rise, and supplies may be limited. (Ambassador comment: It seems likely that these tariffs are a way to make up revenue lost from enormous tax cuts on the very rich in the “One Big Beautiful Bill. It is difficult to understand how higher prices, which will result from these tariffs will improve the American economy.)

On October 10, 2025, Trump threatened to impose a 100% tariff on all Chinese imports starting November 1 in retaliation for China’s newly announced export controls on rare-earth elements. Needless to say, this tariff rate seems likely to have changed once Trump met with Xi during his late October trip to Asia. His justification consisted of a characterization of China as “holding the world captive” by restricting access to technologies used in chips, lasers, jet-engines, etc.

It is likely that these moves will trigger inflation and supply chain disruption, and contribute to global trade instability. (Ambassador comment: As of the morning of 30 October, it seems that Trump has revised down his tariffs. They will still result in higher prices for goods coming out of China. Otherwise, these are great ideas!)

The U.S. ‘s militarized approach in Latin America and unilateral use of force under Trump’s direction is unsettling to smaller democracies in the region which often depend on diplomatic rather than military engagement from the U.S. Per Le Monde, this is “worrying” and might violate international law. 3. Impact on Small, Liberal Democracies (U.S. allies)

Many middle-sized liberal democracies (in Europe, Latin America, Asia) rely on the U.S. for stable trade, security guarantees, multilateral cooperation and leadership in institutions (NATO, WTO, U.N.). Trump’s tilt toward tariffs, unilateral military action and skepticism of multilateralism undermines that foundation.

In Europe or Asia, the threat of a U.S.–China tech trade war places these states in the cross-hairs: they may have to choose between U.S. alignment (and face Chinese economic retaliation) or accommodate China (and upset the U.S.). The unpredictability reduces the confidence smaller democracies can place in the U.S. as a strategic anchor.

Democratic norms: Before Trump, the U.S. was a model for rule-of-law, transparency and institutional stability. A U.S. presidency that appears to suppress transparency (Epstein-files), that deploys domestic and foreign forces beyond oversight, and that pushes labour/trade policy based on unilateralism weakens the global example, emboldening illiberal governments to justify their own erosion of democratic norms.
