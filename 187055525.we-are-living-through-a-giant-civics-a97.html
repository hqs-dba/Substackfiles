Authoritarian Traits

Jim Lawler:
Your book was fascinating—and disturbing. The parallels with 20th‑century authoritarianism were unmistakable. What are the defining traits of these leaders, and do we see them today?

Ruth Ben‑Ghiat:
Until 2016, I studied historic fascism—Mussolini, Nazism, Franco. What struck me during Trump’s 2016 campaign was how many characteristics of strongmen he exhibited. For example, his comment that he could “stand on Fifth Avenue and shoot someone” and not lose followers. That level of impunity rhetoric is a classic strongman hallmark.

These leaders are entirely transactional. They don’t have a fixed moral code. Everyone has utility until they don’t. They hire loyalists and sycophants over expertise. And despite all their bluster, they’re deeply insecure—constantly fearing betrayal—so loyalty must be renewed every day. One misstep, and even the most loyal deputy becomes an enemy.

Sometimes I wonder if dysfunctional relationships with parents play a role in this. Trump’s need for attention, for example.

Ruth Ben‑Ghiat:
Many strongmen had abusive or damaged family relationships. Mussolini was violent from childhood. Others, like Gaddafi, were serial perpetrators of abuse. Trump wanted to please his father but also learned from him—Fred Trump was involved in tax fraud and other schemes. Trump internalized the lesson that being “smart” meant getting away with things. And then he had mentors like Roy Cohn and Roger Stone—individuals skilled in anti‑democratic tactics.

Why Followers Stay Loyal

Jim Lawler:
I’m fascinated by the followers. Why so many people remain loyal despite clear harm—what drives that?

Ruth Ben‑Ghiat:
The key is the moment in which these leaders rise. Strongmen thrive during periods of rapid social change—when some people feel society is shifting away from them: women gaining rights, racial progress, workers’ empowerment. The leader markets himself as the protector, the champion of those who feel displaced.

Many strongmen come from media or advertising. They read the cultural marketplace and present themselves as whatever is most desired at that moment. Followers then feel seen and defended.

Once bonded, it’s hard to break. Shame plays a huge role; when followers start realizing they’ve been misled, they may double down rather than confront that shame—especially if confronted with “I told you so.”

Elites stick with strongmen because they benefit materially and fear losing privilege.

On Disinformation and Deprogramming

Jim Lawler:
Can people be deprogrammed? What breaks the spell?

Ruth Ben‑Ghiat:
While inside the disinformation tunnel, even violence or blatant wrongdoing may not persuade them. But reality eventually intrudes—economic hardship, state brutality, corruption. When the propaganda no longer matches lived experience, cracks form.

But dialogue is essential. We can’t push people further into isolation.

The Authoritarian Doomsday Clock

Jim Lawler:
If there were an “authoritarian doomsday clock,” how close are we to midnight?

Ruth Ben‑Ghiat:
It depends on the metric. Institutional damage—like politicizing DOJ or hollowing out the State Department—is significant. Foreign policy has been personalized, and corruption is rampant. In that sense, we’re far along.

But the judiciary has held firm in many cases—over 300 legal decisions, including by Trump‑appointed judges, have checked authoritarian overreach. Grassroots protests across the country show resilience. Independent media, including Substack, continues to operate.

We’re not past the point of recovery.

Reasons for Hope

Ruth Ben‑Ghiat:
Strongmen often cause their own undoing through overreach. Autocratic backfire happens when corruption becomes blatant, brutality becomes normalized, and hardship becomes widespread. This sparks mass resistance and elite defections.

Never underestimate the American people.

What Citizens Can Do

Jim Lawler:
What can young people—and all of us—do to stem this slide toward authoritarianism?

Ruth Ben‑Ghiat:
We are living through a giant civics lesson. The key is to use every democratic tool we still have—especially elections. People say, “We can’t vote our way out of fascism,” but that’s cynical. Elections still matter. Democrats continue to win key races, which rattles authoritarian actors.

People don’t need to march in the streets to help. Phone‑banking, voter registration, civic engagement—these all make a difference.

And we must embody the values opposite the strongman model: empathy, kindness, community, nonviolence. Strongmen fear solidarity.

Women and Authoritarianism

Jim Lawler:
Your book focuses on strongmen—no strongwomen. Why?

Ruth Ben‑Ghiat:
The book focuses on those who have fully wrecked democracies and ruled as dictators. Historically, that has overwhelmingly been men. But figures like Giorgia Meloni complicate this: she shattered Italy’s glass ceiling while internalizing ideology rooted in Mussolini and Berlusconi. Women can govern as authoritarians too.

Reclaiming Patriotism

Jim Lawler:
You write beautifully about reclaiming patriotic emotion from democracy’s enemies. MAGA shouldn’t have a monopoly on patriotism.

Ruth Ben‑Ghiat:
Exactly. Compassion and love can be democratic virtues, not weaknesses. Authoritarians exploit emotion because democracies often neglect it. We must reclaim it.
