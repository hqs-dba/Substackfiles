The phrase “Fiddling While Rome burns” has returned to the headlines, often used to describe the Trump Administration’s indifference—or worse, opportunism—in the face of national crisis. The expression conjures the image of Nero, the Roman emperor, blithely playing his violin while his capital city was consumed by fire. It has become a shorthand for vanity amid disaster, for a leader so self-absorbed that catastrophe becomes a kind of background music.

But like so many myths, the story says as much about how we remember power as about the events themselves. There was, of course, no fiddle in Nero’s Rome—string instruments of that kind did not exist in the first century A.D. What the Roman historian Tacitus actually reports is that Nero was in his palace singing an epic poem about the destruction of Troy while the flames spread through the city. The symbolism was unmistakable: even as real lives and homes burned, Nero cast himself as a tragic hero in his own performance.

Yet what happened next is even more revealing. Once the fire had done its work—destroying three-quarters of the city—Nero seized the opportunity to clear out the ruins and rebuild the center of Rome according to his own grand design. He claimed vast swaths of the devastated city as imperial property and began construction of his Domus Aurea—the Golden House—a sprawling palace complex of gardens, lakes, and marble halls, adorned with gold leaf and statues of himself.

In other words, Nero wasn’t just fiddling. He was speculating—turning disaster into a development project. He used the destruction as pretext to consolidate power and remake the capital in his image.

That, perhaps, is the real lesson of the metaphor. The danger is not merely that a ruler is indifferent while the city burns, but that he may see in the flames an opportunity: to expand his dominion, to enrich his circle, to rebuild the state as a monument to himself.

Today’s invocation of “Fiddling While Rome burns” should remind us that the problem isn’t only neglect—it’s exploitation. The true authoritarian impulse is not to prevent the fire, but to profit from its aftermath.
