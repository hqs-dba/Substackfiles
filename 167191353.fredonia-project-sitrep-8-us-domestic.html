On June 21, 2025, the United States launched a significant military operation, dubbed "Operation Midnight Hammer," targeting three key Iranian nuclear facilities: Fordo, Natanz, and Isfahan. This action marked a direct U.S. intervention in the escalating conflict between Israel and Iran, with President Donald Trump announcing that the strikes had "totally obliterated" these sites and were a "spectacular military success."

Details of the Operation:

  • Targeted Facilities:

    • Fordo Fuel Enrichment Plant: A heavily fortified, deeply buried uranium enrichment site near Qom. This facility, built deep inside a mountain, was believed to be a critical part of Iran's nuclear program.

    • Natanz Nuclear Facility: Iran's largest enrichment complex, containing vast halls of centrifuges, some underground. Natanz had previously been targeted by Israeli strikes on June 15, which reportedly damaged its above-ground sections and key electricity infrastructure.

    • Isfahan Nuclear Technology Center: A crucial conversion and research facility involved in preparing raw materials for enrichment and reactor use.

  • Military Assets and Execution: The strikes involved a large formation of seven B-2 stealth bombers, launched from the U.S., each armed with GBU-57 Massive Ordnance Penetrator (MOP) "bunker buster" bombs, the world's most powerful. Submarine-launched Tomahawk missiles also struck surface infrastructure targets in Isfahan. The operation, described by the Pentagon as the largest B-2 combat operation in U.S. history, involved more than 125 U.S. aircraft, including fighter jets, tankers, and surveillance aircraft, and utilized over 75 precision-guided weapons. The strikes occurred between 6:40 PM and 7:05 PM EST.

  • Destruction:

    • Satellite Imagery: Satellite images released by Planet Labs PBC and analyzed by Sanad (Al Jazeera's fact-checking agency) and the Associated Press show what appears to be significant damage. The damage is difficult to assess, since most of what needed to be destroyed is underground. At Fordo, images revealed two craters from bunker-busting bombs and a damaged air defense site. Aerial imagery of Fordo also showed six large craters and ash blanketing the mountainside. At Natanz, satellite images showed damage to the above-ground electric substation and pilot fuel enrichment plant, with the subterranean enrichment halls likely "severely damaged if not destroyed altogether" due to power loss, according to the IAEA. Buildings at the Isfahan center were reportedly "torched and destroyed.”

  • Authorization, Consultation and Funding of Military Action:

President Trump made the decision to launch the strikes, with senior officials including Vice President JD Vance, Secretary of State Marco Rubio, and Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth present in the Situation Room as the operation unfolded. General Dan Caine, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, provided details on the execution. However, there was limited prior consultation with Congress and U.S. allies.

  • Authorization and Consultation: President Trump unilaterally authorized the strikes as Commander-in-Chief. He did not seek or receive prior congressional authorization. Instead, the administration informed Congress after the bombers left Iranian airspace. General Caine and Defense Secretary Hegseth stated that the U.S. did not notify other regional partners in advance and only briefed Congress after the strikes were conducted and the B-2 bombers had left Iranian airspace. This lack of advance congressional authorization drew criticism from some lawmakers, who argued it violated the Constitution and the 1973 War Powers Act.

  • The Trump administration's decision to bypass Democrats in the authorization process was largely a continuation of its "go-it-alone" and highly partisan approach to foreign policy, a strategy that risks "saddling his Republican Party with political ownership of the military action.” The administration's rationale, articulated by House Speaker Mike Johnson (R-LA), was that the "imminent danger outweighed the time it would take for Congress to act." (Milatt comment: There was no imminent danger; none. That was rationalization, not reality.). Vice President JD Vance stated, "We're not at war with Iran. We're at war with Iran's nuclear program," attempting to frame the action as a targeted strike rather than a broader conflict requiring a declaration of war. (Milatt comment: VP Vance has served in the US military. It is striking that he does not appear to understand that the US cannot be at war with a policy.   • Some U.S. allies housing American troops were informed as the planes were already in the air. The U.S. did alert Israel ahead of the strikes, and President Trump spoke with Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu following the operation.

  • Funding: While no specific public statements about immediate funding for this particular operation were made, the Trump administration was pushing for an additional $350 billion in national security funds as part of a larger tax breaks bill currently under consideration.. It is presumed that the military action would be funded through existing defense appropriations and potentially supplemented by these new funds.

Effectiveness of Operation Midnight Hammer: Depends on Who is Talking

President Trump’s Assertions:

  • Immediately after "Operation Midnight Hammer," President Trump asserted on Truth Social and in public addresses that the Iranian nuclear facilities at Fordo, Natanz, and Isfahan had been "completely and totally obliterated" and that "monumental damage" had been inflicted. He claimed the "biggest damage took place far below ground level" and that underground tunnels were "all collapsed." (Ambassador comment: Given President Trump’s relationship with fact, Fredonia’s delegation continues to wait for conclusive findings.)

Proof of Destroyed Nuclear Material:

  • Limited Impact on Nuclear Program: While significant damage to above-ground infrastructure and some underground sections was confirmed by satellite imagery, initial intelligence assessments, and later reporting, indicated that the bombs did not "obliterate" Iran's nuclear weapons program or the core of Iran's nuclear capabilities, particularly enriched uranium stockpiles. The Washington Post, citing leaked intelligence, reported growing doubts over the effectiveness of the strikes and "minimal impact" on Iran's nuclear program.

  • The Institute for Science and International Security noted that while enrichment capabilities were severely set back, Iran still retained stockpiles of enriched uranium at various levels, which could be further enriched to weapons-grade if surviving centrifuges were installed elsewhere.

  • Iranian and IAEA Statements: Iran's nuclear agency and the UN nuclear watchdog, the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA), both stated that there was no immediate sign of off-site radioactive contamination near the targeted sites, and no increase in radiation levels was detected. This suggests that any nuclear material, if present, was either contained or moved prior to the strikes as Iranian officials claimed.

  • Experts and intelligence officials largely conclude that the strikes constituted a significant setback to Iran's enrichment capabilities, possibly delaying their program for months or even years, but did not eliminate it.

Not surprisingly, the President rejected any pushback from anyone and characterized the attack as a definitive end to what he dubbed "the 12-day war," akin to the end of World War II, a nonsensical statement at best.

Reactions of World Powers and US Allies:

It appears at this moment that we may have missed the worst (most immediate) retaliation, but it is not entirely unlikely that something will come along later. There were several calls to limit retaliation and more war.

  • Alarm and Calls for Restraint: Most world powers and many U.S. allies reacted with alarm and urged de-escalation and a return to diplomacy.

    • United Nations: Secretary-General António Guterres condemned the use of force, and urged de-escalation, repeating that "there is no military solution."

    • Russia: Strongly condemned the U.S. bombings and Iran's Foreign Minister traveled to Moscow for "serious consultations.”

    • Europe (UK, France, Germany): Responses were measured but cautious. The UK offered conditional support, France expressed "concern" and urged restraint. Germany called on Iran to "immediately begin negotiations."

    • Middle East (beyond Israel): Saudi Arabia expressed "great concern" and called for urgent diplomacy. Iraq and Qatar warned the strikes risked destabilizing an already volatile region. Pakistan condemned the strike, warning of "severely damaging implications."

  • Praise from Israel: Unsurprisingly, Israel lauded the U.S. strikes.

Reactions by Iranians to the Bombs' Effectiveness (Washington Post):

According to reports, including those cited in the Washington Post and other news outlets, the Iranian reaction to the perceived failure of the U.S. bombs to "obliterate" their nuclear program was a mix of defiance, claims of resilience, and warnings of retaliation.

  • Iranian Defiance and Resilience: Iranian officials downplayed the damage, with some claiming the sites had been evacuated in advance and that no irreversible damage was done to critical underground complexes.

  • Iranian Vows of Retaliation: Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) vowed "regrettable responses," emphasizing that the "number, dispersion, and size of U.S. military bases in the region are not a strength, but have doubled their vulnerability."

  • Iranian Increased Paranoia and Internal Security: The Iranian regime reportedly increased counterintelligence measures, and there were reports of arrests of hundreds of Iranians, reflecting the regime's paranoia about infiltration.

Implications for Small, Western Liberal Democratic Allies of the US and US Budget

The U.S. bombing of Iranian nuclear facilities and the subsequent reactions have profound implications for small, Western liberal democratic allies of the U.S.:

  • Increased Instability and Insecurity: The direct U.S. military involvement in the Middle East significantly escalates regional tensions, increased risks of proxy attacks, terrorism, and economic disruption. This instability could spill over into their own domestic security.

  • Strain on Alliances and Multilateralism: President Trump's unilateral decision-making, erodes the trust and effectiveness of existing alliances. Small democracies rely on a strong, predictable, and rules-based international system to remain stable.

  • Economic Vulnerability: Small economies are highly susceptible to the global economic fallout from regional conflicts.

  • Difficult Diplomatic Position: These allies may feel pressured to align with U.S. policy even if it contradicts their own security interests or values.

  • Undermining Democratic Norms: The U.S. administration's actions can set a dangerous precedent and be perceived as undermining democratic norms and the rule of law. This can have a ripple effect, emboldening authoritarian tendencies or weakening democratic institutions within smaller allied nations.

  • Pressure for Increased Defense Spending: If the U.S. signals a reduced commitment to collective security, small allies may face increased pressure to significantly boost their own defense spending, potentially at the expense of social programs or economic development.
