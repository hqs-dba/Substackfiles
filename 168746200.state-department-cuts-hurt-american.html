Damaging support for U.S. business, jobs, and exports is yet another result of the ill-considered cuts at the State Department. American consumers, workers, farmers, and entrepreneurs across the country are going to suffer as a result.

Supporting U.S. exports and American businesses abroad is one of the most basic functions of our embassies and diplomats. It is a specific charge in the letter of instruction that each U.S. ambassador receives from POTUS at the start of their mission. It is an integral part of the work of the staff — U.S.-based and locally engaged — at each U.S. embassy and consulate as well as for the desk officers and others assigned to State Department offices in Washington. Moreover, these public servants work daily with Foreign Service colleagues in the Commerce Department’s Foreign Commercial Service and the FSOs in the U.S. Department of Agriculture as well as with USTR, Treasury, EXIM, DFC, TDA, and other U.S. agencies to increase Americans’ prosperity.

Specifically, our ambassadors, Foreign Service Officers, and locally-engaged staff members

  • Identify specific export opportunities and flag them for U.S companies;

  • Develop market intelligence for every country where the U.S. has relations;

  • Aggressively advocate for U.S. firms with governments and others abroad, including with presidents and prime ministers;

  • Identify unfair, anti-competitive foreign trade and investment practices and work for their removal;

  • Spot and counter export promotion efforts by China and other aggressive competitor countries;

  • Help U.S. companies find great local partners, agents, and other reps;

  • Negotiate for open, fair business conditions for American firms and products;

  • Help American firms in trade disputes and facing unfair foreign government actions;

  • Inspect the quality of certain foods sent to the U.S. to protect American consumers; and

  • Provide consular services to American business people whenever needed.

Yet the July 11 cuts needlessly hurt State’s ability to carry out these jobs. For example, the State Department office that supports and promotes American farmers and agricultural exports is gone. The bureau that advanced Americans’ energy security was eliminated and experienced, highly expert public servants fired. Those are just two of the many, many ill-considered actions executed a week ago.

For Americans’ prosperity and economic well-being, as well as for our overall national security interests, last week’s cuts should be re-examined and in many cases reversed. It is not too late to call back these patriotic public servants.
