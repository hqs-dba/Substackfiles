In the summer of 1989, Panama was under the control of a man who understood the mechanics of power better than he understood the principles of democracy.
General Manuel Noriega had risen from the shadows of intelligence work to become the country’s de facto ruler, perfecting a model of authoritarian control that should feel disturbingly familiar today.

Authoritarianism doesn’t always come with marching boots and midnight coups.
It can creep in through policy changes, institutional “reforms,” and the slow normalization of abuses that citizens tell themselves are temporary.

I’ve seen it firsthand — and I’ve seen where it leads.

Noriega’s playbook was simple and devastatingly effective, mirroring each step of what scholars have outlined as an authoritarian playbook

  1. Politicize independent institutions.
    Courts, electoral commissions, and the media were stacked with loyalists. The law ceased to be an impartial arbiter and became a weapon.

  2. Control the narrative through disinformation.
    State media and paid outlets spread lies about political opponents, sowing confusion until truth itself became suspect.

  3. Expand executive power at the expense of checks and balances.
    Noriega ruled by decree, sidelined the legislature, and turned the military into his personal enforcement arm.

  4. Quash criticism and dissent.
    Opposition leaders were exiled, silenced, or worse. The press was censored. Protesters met with tear gas and truncheons.

  5. Scapegoat the vulnerable.
    Marginalized communities, student activists, and labor leaders were painted as foreign agents and enemies of the state.

  6. Corrupt the electoral process.
    Fraud wasn’t a last resort; it was a standard operating procedure. The 1984 election was stolen in broad daylight.

  7. Stoke violence to maintain fear.

  8. Paramilitary “Dignity Battalions” roamed the streets, intimidating anyone who dared resist.

If this list sounds eerily relevant in 2025, it’s because the patterns are timeless.

Today, in the wake of a deeply polarized U.S. presidential election, we are seeing movements within our government that mirror these tactics: loyalty tests for judges, coordinated disinformation campaigns, the sidelining of oversight bodies, attacks on the press, demonization of immigrants and minorities, efforts to undermine election integrity, and political rhetoric that condones — even encourages — violence .

In Noriega’s Panama, these steps didn’t happen all at once. They unfolded gradually, often wrapped in the language of national security and patriotism. Each breach of democratic norms became the “new normal,” until the rule of law existed only on paper.

And that is the quiet danger of an authoritarian transition: people acclimate to it.
They stop noticing the incremental shifts. They stop resisting, not because they agree, but because they’ve adapted.

It took an armed U.S. intervention — Operation Just Cause — to finally remove Noriega from power. Panama’s institutions had been so compromised that internal democratic correction was impossible.

Not every nation gets a second chance.

In the decades since, we’ve seen similar trajectories in Venezuela and Nicaragua — leaders dismantling institutional safeguards, controlling information, and using fear to consolidate power. Once these patterns take hold, reversing them is exponentially harder.

The lesson is clear: Democracy doesn’t collapse overnight. It dies in slow motion — in procedural tweaks, in the silencing of dissent, in the normalization of lies. The only defense is recognizing the pattern early and refusing to accept its inevitability.

History doesn’t just repeat itself. Sometimes, it holds up a mirror.
The question is whether we are willing to look into it — and whether we’ll act before the reflection becomes reality.
