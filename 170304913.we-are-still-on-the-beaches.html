We Are Still On The Beaches

America today stands at a precipice, facing an existential threat not from external adversaries, but from the insidious creep of autocratic forces within. While the allure of imagining a future "rebuilding" is strong, it is a dangerous fantasy to indulge in when the very foundations of our democratic republic are under assault. We are not yet at a point where we can plan for reconstruction; we are, in fact, at the stage where the fight for survival is paramount.

Winston Churchill, with his unparalleled understanding of the stakes involved in the fight against tyranny, recognized the necessity of absolute, unflinching resistance. In the face of imminent invasion, he rallied his nation with a clarion call that resonates profoundly with our current predicament:

We shall go on to the end, we shall fight in France, we shall fight on the seas and oceans, we shall fight with growing confidence and growing strength in the air, we shall defend our Island, whatever the cost may be, we shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields and in the streets, we shall fight in the hills; we shall never surrender.

This was not a speech about rebuilding; it was a dramatic, desperate plea for a nation to stand its ground, even in the face of potentially ill-fated resistance. Today, the beaches are metaphorical, representing the institutions of our democracy – our electoral processes, our independent judiciary, our free press – all under assault by those who seek to dismantle the very checks and balances that define our republic. To speak of rebuilding now is to concede defeat on these beaches before the battle is truly joined.

Only much later, after years of brutal conflict and against a backdrop of the tide slowly turning, could Churchill articulate a cautious optimism. Following the victory at El Alamein, a crucial turning point, he famously remarked, "Now this is not the end. It is not even the beginning of the end. But it is, perhaps, the end of the beginning."

This statement, while hopeful, still underscored the immense struggle that lay ahead. It was a recognition that the struggle had reached a tipping point, but by no means was the war over. In America's current struggle, we are far from this "end of the beginning." We have not yet secured decisive victories against the forces of autocracy; instead, we are witnessing their continued consolidation of power and influence.

The shift towards planning for a postwar world, exemplified by meetings like the Yalta Conference, occurred only after the defeat of Nazi Germany was a near certainty. At Yalta, the Allied leaders met to discuss the post-war order, the division of Europe, and the foundations of a new international system. This was a gathering predicated on the understanding that the war would, in fact, be won, and that the time for rebuilding and restructuring was at hand. We are not at our Yalta moment. We are not discussing the future of our nation after the defeat of autocratic forces; instead, we are grappling with the very real possibility of their ascendancy.

While we must certainly have a vision for the future, to focus on "rebuilding" in America today is a dangerous distraction. It presupposes a victory that has not yet been secured and, more critically, deflects from the urgent need to resist the present threat. Nor are we at the cautious optimism of "the end of the beginning"; we are, unequivocally, at the point of fighting on the beaches. The struggle for the soul of America is not a distant, imagined future; it is here and now, demanding our immediate and unwavering resolve. Only after we have secured our democratic foundations can we truly begin to envision and enact the work of rebuilding.

Cash served as a former prosecutor in the Manhattan District Attorney's office before joining the CIA in 1994 as Assistant General counsel and subsequently serving as an intelligence officer in the Directorate of Operations. In 2001 he joined the Senate Select committee on Intelligence as Counsel and designee-staffer to Senator Diane Feinstein). He later served as a senior staffer in the House Select Committee on Homeland Security, the Department of Energy, the Senate Judiciary Subcommittee on Terrorism, Technology and Homeland Security and the Department of Energy. In the private sector he has advised on national security, counterintelligence, and technology policy and served on the Biological Sciences Experts Group under the Director of National Intelligence. Mr. Cash is currently the Executive Director of The Steady State.

Founded in 2016, The Steady State is a nonpartisan, nonprofit 501(c)(4) organization of more than 300 former senior national security professionals. Our membership includes former officials from the CIA, FBI, Department of State, Department of Defense and Department of Homeland Security. Drawing on deep expertise across national security disciplines including intelligence, diplomacy, military affairs and law, we advocate for constitutional democracy, the rule of law and the preservation of America’s national security institutions.
