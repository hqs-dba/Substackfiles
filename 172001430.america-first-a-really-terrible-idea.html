The Trump Administration is fond of declaring that its motto is “America First.” The idea seems OK on the surface. Who, after all, would be for “America Second”? But it is actually both an empty slogan – what does the phrase even mean? – and an insidious mask for an arrogant and ignorant but nevertheless triumphalist approach to the world and America’s place in it.

In many ways – the American economy is the largest in the world, the dollar has been the world’s reserve currency, the U.S. hosts everyone else at the United Nations headquarters in New York, and American influence is felt in every corner of the world and in discussion of almost every international issue – the U.S. has been first for a long time.

The corollary idea of “America First” – that the U.S. has somehow been fooled by others and must regain some imaginary lost status – is a pernicious illusion. It’s just not true. In fact, the Trump Administration’s efforts – declaring unnecessary trade wars, snubbing Allies, pooh-poohing their interests, demanding fealty and exaggerated “gratitude” from all and sundry while itself kowtowing to adversaries, fumbling one difficult international issue after another, savagely dismantling U.S. capabilities to deal with those issues – have actually weakened and endangered America. Far from putting America first, it has ignored the national interest even when it has understood it, in favor of greed, wishful thinking, and short-term personal gain, all the while imagining itself holding, as Trump likes to think of it, the high cards. By arrogantly quitting an international organization and a potentially cooperative undertaking one after another, it has pointlessly given away the U.S.' place at the head of the table.

There is even something deceptive about the notion of regarding misfortune, tragedy, and poverty outside the U.S. as secondary to Americans’ own needs. To be sure, in my thirty-some years as a U.S. diplomat, promoting U.S. interests to serve the security and well-being of the American people was at the heart of my job description. But there is a crucial fact here that the current Administration, with its promotion of “America First,” not only willfully disregards, but scorns:

We are not alone in the world.

Others’ interests, actions, and needs affect our own. Sometimes, we can control the effects; other times, we can’t. Consider an area where “America First” is particularly nonsensical. To pretend that our borders can ward off transnational crises like climate change and the spread of disease is foolish and official malpractice. And consider this: Scientists have now established a link all over the world between rising temperatures and increased risks to pregnant women and their babies. Increased heat-related rates of miscarriage; premature birth; various dangers to mothers (before and after birth); and dangers to infants under neo-natal care have now been documented. Imagining that the U.S. can somehow be shielded from these risks is foolish.

To many Americans, “America First’s” primary significance is in the domestic realm – the idea that the U.S. should first look after its own. To the extent that Americans’ needs have not been adequately addressed, but seem to be getting new attention now, those Americans see the phrase as a worthy idea. But even this seemingly benign notion is an illusion. Americans’ needs are not currently being addressed: the Administration’s slashing of Federal services and benefits removes things on which Americans rely. These services and benefits are not gravy for “undeserved” groups or useful but not vital supplements for others better off. Anyone who insists they are is actually kidding him or herself and anyone listening.

“America First” is not a new concept. It has a long history and has been used before as a deceptive slogan to fan isolationism. Influential voices argued vigorously in the 1930s and early 1940s that the rise of aggressively dangerous dictatorships abroad should be of no concern to the United States. While “keeping America out of other people’s quarrels” might have seemed desirable on the surface, it was a fatal misreading of the danger those quarrels posed to the U.S.

Even worse, many “America Firsters” secretly took Nazi money to spread Nazi propaganda (devoted as it was to deterring the U.S. from taking action against either Japan or Germany) and represented it as their own message. It took the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor, and 4000 American dead, to loosen the “America Firster” grip on large segments of American opinion. Lest we think that such interference by foreign adversaries couldn’t happen now, it’s worth remembering that the Justice Department indicted in September 2024 two Russians for funneling large sums to a right-wing media outlet, which apparently never asked itself whose money it was taking. The outlet’s journalists and influencers all protested, in turn, that they had been “duped” into pocketing those funds to produce and distribute videos aligned with Russian propaganda. Can anyone really be that naïve?

Thinking of one’s own nation’s needs in isolation is a tragically mistaken approach; Cooperative international action is called for. Cooperation with others magnifies, not diminishes, and strengthens, not weakens, American standing and power.

Tom Wolfson is a former senior U.S. diplomat who has lived and worked in six foreign countries, occasionally multiple times. His work representing the U.S. has included assignments at the United Nations, in the U.S. Congress, and with an international democracy-building organization. He is a member of The Steady State.

Founded in 2016, The Steady State is a nonpartisan, nonprofit 501(c)(4) organization of more than 300 former senior national security professionals. Our membership includes former officials from the CIA, FBI, Department of State, Department of Defense and Department of Homeland Security. Drawing on deep expertise across national security disciplines including intelligence, diplomacy, military affairs and law, we advocate for constitutional democracy, the rule of law and the preservation of America’s national security institutions.
