The fatal ambush of two West Virginia National Guard soldiers near the White House last week has unleashed a wave of inflammatory claims, including public assertions by senior officials that the Afghan suspect entered the United States with “zero vetting.” That allegation is not simply misleading — it is false.

According to multiple reporting streams and individuals familiar with the case, the individual was screened before entry, consistent with the processes applied to all Afghan evacuees. The rush to declare a “vetting failure” before facts are established is not a trivial mistake; it is a politically motivated distortion that undermines public trust in the very national-security systems designed to protect the country. The truth is more complex — and far more important — than the narrative now being pushed.

Screening and vetting (S&V) are among the most technically demanding and consequential functions in the national-security enterprise. While the processes themselves are complex, three foundational principles govern their effectiveness:

  1. Data quality is foundational.

    The strength of any S&V system depends on the richness and completeness of U.S. Government databases on known and suspected terrorists—biographic identifiers, biometric signatures, and derogatory information.

  2. More information produces more accurate assessments.

    The more detail available about an individual, the better the S&V community can determine whether that person poses a concern.

  3. Time enables rigor.

    When analysts have sufficient time, they can conduct deeper reviews, pursue additional leads, and resolve ambiguities that would otherwise remain unanswered.

These principles shaped every stage of the Afghanistan evacuation.

Every individual evacuated from Afghanistan underwent at least a biographic and biometric check against Government databases. For some evacuees — such as Special Immigrant Visa (SIV) applicants, refugee applicants, and Afghans with extensive prior work for U.S.-affiliated organizations — the United States already possessed a significant amount of information. Many had been screened and vetted well before Kabul fell. These cases represented the most complete and reliable pre-evacuation vetting.

But thousands of Afghans who reached evacuation aircraft did so with little or no documentation and under extreme time pressure. For these individuals, the initial biographic and biometric checks were all that could be completed during the evacuation itself. There was no realistic opportunity to gather additional information or conduct more in-depth analysis before airlift.

Recognizing these constraints, the U.S. Government implemented additional layers of screening and vetting after evacuees reached intermediate staging locations or the United States. These measures included:

  • Enhanced information collection to fill gaps in identity, family background, and prior activities.

  • Analytic tools such as call-chaining to identify potential links to individuals in terrorist databases.

  • Continuous vetting, where legally permissible, to detect new derogatory information if and when it appears.

These steps reflected the same core S&V principles: data, information depth, and time. What could not be resolved during the compressed urgency of evacuation was addressed through a structured, multi-layered process afterward.

Since the failures exposed on 9/11, screening and vetting have steadily improved. The system is far from perfect — identity intelligence is inherently challenging, and adversaries continuously evolve their methods. But the United States has invested heavily in strengthening this capability, and it has worked as intended.

It is essential to remember that attacks carried out by refugees or new arrivals have almost never resulted from S&V failures. When attacks have occurred, they typically stem from radicalization after arrival, not from flaws in the initial screening.

That is why investments in S&V must be paired with investments in the stability and wellbeing of those we admit. Newly arrived individuals often face economic hardship, isolation, and uncertainty — conditions that can strain families, increase vulnerability, and complicate integration. Supporting them is not only humane; it is also a national-security imperative.

One such effort, the Afghan Adjustment Act, was designed to provide Afghans who supported U.S. efforts during the war a pathway to lawful permanent residency. It would offer stability to individuals who risked their lives alongside American personnel. Although introduced multiple times in Congress, it has yet to pass — an omission that continues to undermine both our national-security interests and our moral obligations.
